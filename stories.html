Liam

The Journey

"As you know, my name is Liam. I am a trans guy. I’m post-op now, one year post-op my top surgery. I am 1 year post-op of my hysterectomy. I do a lot of educational videos about the trans culture, and advocating for the trans community on youtube, and as well as podcasts, though not just about trans related things, but LGBT issues and topics in general.

So when I first came to Vancouver, BC of August 2015, I came to Vancouver for a Disney audition, because I do a lot of acting. At the time, I had the audition, everything went through, everything was fine. And then they found out I was a trans guy.

So the opportunity I got through that, it was taken away from me, which left me with no money, no place to go anymore. So I found Covenant House, that was located on Pender Street Downtown Vancouver. I was there for 5 or 6 weeks, I was there at Covenant House on their program, which was interesting. It was definitely hard, especially being a trans guy.

Cause I was still new to the whole thing, like early stages on hormones. At that time, I didn't have top surgery. It was a little awkward being being an all guy shelter. At times, it made me feel really overwhelmed and uncomfortable, so I was there for 5 to 6 weeks. [I] landed a job, lost that job, and then I was back on the streets for around month and a half with my friend, which was a lot harder, because we couldn't get in a shelter.

So what we had to do, is, we had to purchase a tent. And for safety we would walk all the way to North Van, by fibbs exchange. We made like a little campsite under the bridge for us, and at that time, two cats. So it was me and my buddy, who's also a trans guy, with two cats in a tent, which was really hard. But we didn't let that stop us, we were working still.

So it was really hard, because we had to charge our phones at like Tims the night before, so we could have an alarm. Get up, we would have to walk to like North Van for the seabus, seabus to Vancouver, then take a bus to work. Then we would have to walk home.

But we did that, saved up all our money, and then we got an apartment in Gastown.

On the hardest thing

It was definitely hard being in that position, being on the streets is never an easy thing, and it's scary, for your safety, especially in the lower mainland area. There's a lot of drug use, and it's really easy to get hooked on those things, because you have a lot of different things going on in your head, like anxiety. I think that's when I started getting more anxiety and depression, because I didn't understand why I was missing out on opportunities just because I was trans-- it didn't make sense, and it still doesn't make sense to me.

And actually recently, I had an audition with Disney again, and even though I was trans, they still wanted me, which was ironic, because before they said no, but now that I have surgery, it's okay. So still that makes no sense to me, but it was really hard every day in life and just feel like people are looking at you differently, because A) you're on the streets and not financially stable, and on top of that, you're trans and you're wanting to get your shit together, but then you can't because people look down on you, because of what you identify with. So that was the hardest part.

To cope with anxiety, at that time, I smoked a lot of pot to help with anxiety. To be honest, I still do that. I do have my green card, like it's medicated marijuana. So I smoke that to help. And I've been diagnosed with prescription for citalopram which helps with anxiety as well. I'll be going back on that too, so yayy.

On family

When I first came to Canada from the UAE, my family didn't really want anything to do with me, for being what I identify as.

Financially, no. At that time, I was talking with my biological mother, because we weren't talking for a few years at that time. Especially when I first came out, she was highly against it, but now we've kind of fixed things-- we're actually communicating now. At that time, my father was not really talking to me when I was going through this, so they weren't financially helping me, cause we weren't talking, because they didn't really understand why I transitioned. Basically they didn't understand the full concept, so no at that time they were not someone I could talk to or someone that could help me out in that situation, so I was basically on my own.

So I was trying to fix things on my own and use resources that I was finding out on how to get back on my feet again.

On the topic of home

I guess, for me, since like I've travelled a lot. I've been in positions where I was really good financially, wehre I was mediocore financially, and where I was starting from scratch all over again.
So, for me, home is like where the heart is, I know it's cliche and I know it's a common saying. But for me, home is not like a luxerious house or having all the latest furniture, TV. If you have good friends and people who genuinely love you, then to me, that's home.

I could be just in a tent and have good people around me, and that would be considered home to me. So that's what I would say for that.

On the topic of changes in world view

World View. I've learned from being in that situation that I'm a lot more streets smart than I thought and I'm a lot more alert than I thought. I mean the first time that I was kind of thrown to the street, I was scared. I didn't know where to go and the first where I had to go to the shelter, I was by myself. I had no cell phone, I had no friends. I thought I had a friend, but he was nowhere to be seen. So it was really scary for me, so I was really depressed for a bit, but it was getting later on the day, so my mission was like I'm not going to be on the street. I had nothing, like literally nothing in my bookbag.

So I went to the library and I was checking for shelters and where I could stay, and just resources of what I can do. That's when I found Covenent House, and I was able to give them a phone call and they actually had space. So I went there and they did the whole clearance thing, and make sure I was clean and made sure I didn't have any drugs like that.

On learning

I learned the value of a dollar. I learned the value of a dollar. Because I grew up in Dubai, even now, I'm still trying to teach myself budgeting and stuff. Because there, it's a very rich country. There I started working at a young age, and I was getting a lot of money at a young age, so I was used to just having money to spend. Since recently, being in the situation where I was on the streets [], again, I've been trying to teach myself to budget and learning the value of a dollar, which is still something I'm still trying to learn and try to work towards.

It's because there people just throw money, and that's what I was used to, the lifestyle. Here, it's expensive and taxes and everything you earn is taken from you. So I would say I learned the value of a dollar and learned to appreciate what I have, even when it's not a lot, and just really appreciate it.

On support for homeless (youth)

Absolutely not. So many people are turned away from shelters. It's kind of heartbreaking.

I understand it gets full and they don't really want junkies and stuff like that going to these places. The issue that I find that I've actually encountered is people who are genuinely did not put themselves on the streets, it's just due to whatever reason, those are the people that get turned away the most from shelters.

If you're not hooked onto something or you're not hooked on something, or if you're not like puking or whatever, then you get turned away from shelters because they think you just want a free ride, free food stuff like that. So I don't think there's enough resources for people who actually deserve it, if that makes any sense.

Like there's so many shelters, especially in Vancouver. And the sad thing is they can't accommodate everyone or they turn away people, people that are actually trying to get their life together, and get work, and save up money and find a house. So I don't think there are enough resources for that.

On behind the scenes reasons

Well, yes, I'm Canadian and I spent a lot of time in the Middle East and grew up most of my life in the UAE in Dubai, which is one of the world's richest countries, and I'm priviledged to have lived there and to kind of grow up there.

The main reason that I came back to Canada that I kind of gave up everything, because I had my career, I had my friends, i was steady, financially I was secure. But I chose to get rid of everything and drop everything because I was so detached from my body and I didn't feel right and I was so depressed. I was getting money, at the end of the day, money is just paper, well in Canada, plastic. And I was just buying random shit to kind of making feel better. It was just a constant cycle. I was drinking every day and was just drinking a lot. I would blow two grand a day at a pool bar, just drinking with friends. I was just wasn't happy.

The main reason that I came back to Canada was to be who I knew I was, to transition, to get on hormone therapy, to go through my surgeries, and just to be who I knew that I was.

I wanted to meet my biological father, which was another reason why I came to Canada, but the main reason was to transition and kind of get away from that and kind of start over again.

I kind of wish that I was more wise with money, because I did not take into consideration surgeries. And when you are on surgery, you have to take time off of work. So when I was off of hysterectomy I was off for five months, wasn't getting any money from that, because even though I'm Canadian, I can't get EI or welfare or anything like that, because I haven't been back in Canada long enough, because I left Canada when I was a minor. That was shitty, because people were telling me I could get EI, but I couldn't. Then I had top surgery last January, and I had to take around 6 and half almost 7 months off work. So that was hard. But now I'm working full time, so now things are slowly picking up.
